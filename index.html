FLOOD RISK MAPPING - SHILABATI WATERSHED

Comprehensive Flood Hazard Assessment

This flood Risk map of the Shilabati watershed integrates multiple parameters including elevation, slope, drainage density, rainfall intensity, land use/land cover, and soil permeability to identify areas vulnerable to flooding. The analysis uses GIS-based Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA).

Key Findings:

Low to Very Low Flood Risk Zones: The upper and north-western parts of the Shilabati watershed fall under very low to low flood risk due to higher elevation, moderate drainage density, and dominance of 1st and 2nd order streams with limited discharge. Rapid runoff dispersion minimizes flood accumulation in these areas.

Moderate Hazard Zones: The central portion of the watershed represents a transitional zone characterized by 3rd and 4th order streams, moderate slopes, and increased flow accumulation. These conditions enhance surface runoff concentration, making the area moderately vulnerable during intense rainfall events.

High Flood Risk Zones:The southern and south-eastern downstream sections exhibit high to very high flood risk, closely associated with 4th and 5th order streams. Low relief, wide floodplains, and tributary convergence make these zones highly susceptible to frequent monsoonal flooding.

Critical very high Flood-Prone Areas: Very high-risk zones are clustered along the main Shilabati River and major tributaries, where prolonged waterlogging, bank overflow, and flood damage are most severe, indicating priority areas for flood mitigation planning.

LANDSLIDE SUSCEPTIBILITY MAPPING - SIKKIM

Landslide Susceptibility Assessment

This landslide susceptibility map for Sikkim incorporates critical factors including Soil texture, Stream Power Index, Topographic wetness Index, geomorphology, LS factors, rainfall intensity, LULC, drainage density, distances from road, river, settlement, Slope aspect, curvature and existing landslide scurs. The analysis uses Analytical Hierarchy Process (AHP) methods.

Key Findings:

Very high-risk zones: (red) are spatially clustered along major valleys, road corridors, and steep escarpments. These areas are extremely prone to frequent landslides, especially during monsoon rainfall, and require immediate monitoring and mitigation measures.

High Susceptibility Zones: (orange) are observed mainly in the southern and south-western sections. These zones are associated with steeper slopes, road cuts, weathered lithology, and higher surface runoff, increasing slope instability.

Moderate Susceptibility Zones: (yellow) are widely distributed across the central parts of the region. These zones reflect transitional terrain with moderate slopes, mixed land use, and increasing drainage influence, making them vulnerable during intense rainfall events.

Very Low to Low Susceptibility Zones:(dark and light green) are mainly concentrated in the northern and north-eastern parts of Sikkim. They correspond to relatively stable terrain, gentler slopes, dense vegetation cover, and lower human intervention.

EARTHQUAKE VULNERABILITY ZONATION - INDIA

Seismic Vulnerability Assessment in India

This comprehensive earthquake vulnerability zonation map integrates Historical earthquake epicenters recorded prior to the current assessment period.

Seismic Zones & Vulnerability:

Zone V(Very High Vulnerability Zones): Northeast India, parts of Jammu & Kashmir, Himachal Pradesh, and Uttarakhand. Peak Ground Acceleration (PGA) values exceed 0.36g.

Zone IV(High to Moderate Vulnerability Zones): Delhi, parts of Bihar, West Bengal, and Gujarat. PGA values range from 0.24g to 0.36g.

Zone III(Low Vulnerability Zones): Parts of Maharashtra, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh. PGA values range from 0.16g to 0.24g.

Zone II( Very Low Vulnerability Zones): Southern peninsula and central India. PGA values less than 0.16g.

Key Vulnerable Areas:

The analysis identifies 35 major cities in Zone IV and V with high population density and vulnerable building stock.

AGRICULTURAL DROUGHT MONITORING -KANGSABATI WATERSHED(2018)

Drought Vulnerability Assessment using Remote Sensing

This agricultural drought monitoring analysis for Kangsabati Watershed utilizes satellite-derived indices including Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) daviation, Vegetation Condition Index (VCI) daviaton, Temperature Condition Index (TCI), and rainfall daviaton for the period 2000-2023.

Drought Severity Classification:

Extreme Drought: 18% of agricultural land showing severe moisture stress and crop failure during 2018 drought events.

Severe Drought: 25% of area with significant crop water stress and yield reduction by 40-60%.

Moderate Drought: 32% of agricultural land experiencing periodic water scarcity affecting crop growth.

Normal Conditions: 25% of area with adequate soil moisture and normal vegetation health.

Critical Findings:

The analysis reveals increasing drought frequency from 2018 to present indicating climate change impacts on agricultural sustainability.

AIR QUALITY INDEX (AQI) ANALYSIS - DELHI NATIONAL CAPITAL REGION (NCR)

AQI distribution across Delhi NCR showing pollution hotspots and monitoring stations
Comprehensive Air Quality Assessment for Delhi NCR

This Air Quality Index (AQI) analysis for Delhi NCR evaluates pollution levels using real-time monitoring data for PM2.5, PM10, NO₂, SO₂, CO, and O₃ pollutants from 2018-2023. Delhi NCR consistently ranks among the world's most polluted urban regions, with AQI frequently exceeding hazardous levels during winter months.
